Introduction: India’s Trade in Numbers

India crossed the USD 1 trillion merchandise trade milestone in 2021–22. Total exports reached approximately USD 422 billion in 2022–23, while imports exceeded USD 714 billion — a trade deficit of USD 265 billion, larger than the entire GDP of many middle-income countries. This persistent deficit is structural: it is driven by India’s energy import dependence, its under-representation in manufactured goods exports, and its growing import demand for industrial inputs.

India’s trade coverage ratio — exports as a percentage of imports — has declined from around 70% in the early 2000s to approximately 55–60% in recent years, indicating that import growth has consistently outpaced export growth.

Cereal Exports: India as the World’s Rice Granary

Code 
cereals <- qty[HSCode2==10]
cereals[,Commodity:=fcase(HSCode4=="1006","Rice",HSCode4=="1001","Wheat",
  HSCode4=="1005","Maize",HSCode4=="1008","Millet & Buckwheat",default="Other Cereals")]
cer_yr <- cereals[,.(Qty=sum(Export,na.rm=TRUE)),by=.(Year,Commodity)]

  levels=c("Other Cereals","Millet & Buckwheat","Maize","Wheat","Rice"))]

India is the world’s largest rice exporter, accounting for ~40% of global rice trade. The data shows wheat exports peaking in 2021–22 when global prices were high, before being sharply curtailed by an export ban in May 2022 to protect domestic supplies.

Petroleum (HS Chapter 27) is India’s single largest import category, constituting 25–30% of total import value. India’s crude oil import dependence (85% of consumption from overseas) makes every USD 10/bbl increase in crude prices add approximately USD 14–15 billion to the annual import bill.

Conclusion

The trade data paints a clear picture: India has become a significant global trader, but its import basket remains dominated by three categories where structural dependence is high — petroleum, fertilizers, and edible oils. These three together account for over 40% of total import value and are all subject to large geopolitical and commodity price risks. Meanwhile, export growth has been led by petroleum products (from refinery capacity), engineering goods, and chemicals — but manufactured goods’ share remains well below India’s aspiration to become a leading manufacturing exporter. Closing this gap is the central trade policy challenge of the coming decade.
