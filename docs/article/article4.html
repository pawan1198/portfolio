Introduction: A Nation That Cannot Feed Itself Cooking Oil

Walk into any kitchen in India — from a modest rural home in Rajasthan to a high-rise apartment in Mumbai — and you will find at least one or two bottles of edible oil. Cooking oil is indispensable to Indian cuisine. It is the medium through which spices bloom, vegetables are sautéed, and puris are fried. Yet for all its culinary centrality, India produces far less edible oil than it consumes. Every year, the country imports between 14 and 16 million tonnes of vegetable oils — spending over USD 15–20 billion in foreign exchange — to bridge this gap. The story of India’s edible oil import dependence is not merely an agricultural policy failure; it is a story of geopolitical vulnerability, household inflation, and a decades-long policy reckoning that has yet to be fully resolved.

This article traces the evolution of India’s edible oil import basket from the oil year 2010–11 to 2023–24, examines the structural factors driving import dependence, explores the concentration risks embedded in India’s current supply chains, and assesses the policy levers available to reduce this dependence over the coming decade.

The Scale of the Problem: From Self-Sufficiency to Import Addiction

In the 1980s, India undertook the Technology Mission on Oilseeds (TMO), which dramatically expanded oilseed cultivation and reduced import dependence for a brief period. By the early 1990s, India came close to self-sufficiency in edible oils. But this achievement was short-lived. As incomes rose through the liberalisation era, per capita edible oil consumption began to climb steadily — from roughly 5 kg per capita per year in the 1980s to over 20 kg today. Domestic oilseed area expansion and yield improvements could not keep pace with this consumption surge.

The data from Ministry of Commerce records shows that by 2010–11, India was already importing approximately 8.7 million tonnes of edible oils annually. By 2023–24, this figure had crossed 16.5 million tonnes — an increase of nearly 90% over fourteen years. The import bill grew even faster because the surge in import volumes coincided with a global commodity supercycle and, critically, with the Russia–Ukraine war of 2022, which triggered an extraordinary price spike across all three major oil categories.

India’s self-sufficiency ratio — domestic edible oil availability as a percentage of total requirement — has been stuck below 50% for most of the past two decades. This means India depends on foreign sources for more than half of every litre of cooking oil consumed in the country. This is a structural vulnerability of the first order.

The Three-Oil Basket: Palm, Soybean, and Sunflower

India’s edible oil imports are dominated by three oil types, each sourced from a distinct geographic corridor and driven by its own demand and price dynamics.

Palm Oil: The Dominant but Declining Force

Palm oil has historically accounted for the largest share of India’s edible oil imports — as high as 79% of total import volumes in 2010–11. This extraordinary dominance reflects palm oil’s cheapness (it is the world’s lowest-cost edible oil per unit of calorie), its versatility in both cooking and food processing, and its ready availability from Indonesia and Malaysia, which together account for roughly 85% of global palm oil production.

By 2023–24, however, palm oil’s share in India’s import basket had declined to approximately 56%. This is not because volumes fell — palm oil imports remained substantial. Rather, it reflects the faster growth of imports of sunflower oil and, to a lesser extent, soybean oil. Indian consumers, particularly in urban areas, have been shifting away from palm oil toward “lighter” oils perceived as healthier. Simultaneously, food manufacturers began diversifying their oil inputs in response to palm oil price volatility.

The geographic concentration of palm oil supply remains a critical risk. Indonesia and Malaysia combined consistently supply over 90% of India’s palm oil imports. Any disruption to their export policy — Indonesia imposed a devastating palm oil export ban in April 2022, triggering a domestic shortage and price spike in India — directly impacts Indian household food costs within weeks.

Soybean Oil: The Volatile Middle Child

Soybean oil occupies a fluctuating share of 15–30% in India’s edible oil import basket, driven largely by South American supply dynamics. Argentina is the primary supplier, typically providing over 70% of India’s soybean oil imports. Brazil plays a secondary role, with its share rising in years when Argentine production is hit by drought or other supply disruptions.

The Argentina–Brazil duopoly creates its own concentration risk. El Niño-related droughts in the La Plata Basin have repeatedly caused sharp reductions in Argentine soybean production, forcing India to source from Brazil at higher prices or accept domestic oil price increases. The 2022–23 season, which saw one of the worst Argentine droughts on record, is a case in point: soybean oil prices in India spiked sharply as Argentine supplies tightened.

Sunflower Oil: The Fast-Growing New Entrant

The most striking structural shift in India’s edible oil import basket is the rise of sunflower oil. For most of the period from 2010–11 to 2019–20, sunflower oil accounted for only 6–14% of imports. By 2023–24, its share had surged to approximately 22%. Indian consumers — particularly health-conscious urban middle-class households — have embraced sunflower oil as a “light” and relatively neutral-flavoured oil suitable for Indian cooking.

The vast majority of India’s sunflower oil imports come from Ukraine and Russia, which together dominate global sunflower oil production and export. India’s growing reliance on this corridor was brutally exposed in February 2022, when Russia’s invasion of Ukraine triggered an immediate supply disruption. Ukraine’s Black Sea export corridors were blockaded; sunflower oil prices tripled within weeks. India scrambled to source alternative supplies, but the episode underscored a fundamental vulnerability: India had allowed its sunflower oil supply to become almost entirely dependent on a single geopolitically volatile corridor.

The 2021–22 Price Shock: A Stress Test of India’s Oil Supply Chain

The financial year 2021–22 served as a brutal stress test for India’s edible oil supply chains. A confluence of three simultaneous shocks drove edible oil prices to multi-decade highs. First, the pandemic-era demand recovery in major consuming countries pushed global edible oil demand sharply upward. Second, poor palm oil harvests in Malaysia due to labour shortages (the plantation sector relies heavily on migrant workers who had returned home during COVID) reduced global supply. Third, and most critically, Russia’s invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 disrupted both sunflower oil supplies from the Black Sea corridor and soybean production in Argentina (via knock-on commodity market effects).

India’s total edible oil import bill crossed USD 20 billion in 2021–22 — an extraordinary expenditure that represented a severe drain on foreign exchange reserves and contributed directly to consumer price inflation. The retail price of mustard oil in Delhi touched ₹200 per litre; palm oil, the cheapest staple, crossed ₹140 per litre. For low-income households, which spend 30–40% of their food budget on edible oils, this was a severe income shock.

The government responded with emergency measures: reducing import duties on crude edible oils to zero, banning refined edible oil imports (to protect domestic refiners while ensuring crude oil supply), and releasing strategic reserves. These measures provided some relief, but they illustrated the fundamental problem — India had no deep strategic reserve of edible oils (unlike China, which maintains multi-month reserves), and its domestic production base was too small to provide a meaningful buffer.

Why Domestic Production Has Failed to Keep Pace

The failure of Indian oilseed production to meet domestic demand is rooted in multiple structural constraints. First, oilseed crops in India are predominantly rainfed and grown on marginal land, with yields far below global averages. India’s soybean yield is approximately 1.0–1.2 MT per hectare — roughly half the Brazilian level and a fraction of the US level. Mustard yields are similarly low by international standards.

Second, oilseed cultivation is in direct competition with cereals — primarily wheat and paddy — which are supported by far more robust procurement, price support, and input subsidy systems. Farmers have a strong rational incentive to grow wheat and paddy, which can be sold to government agencies at guaranteed MSP prices, over oilseeds, which must be sold in private markets at more volatile prices.

Third, the oil palm cultivation programme, which could theoretically provide the highest oil-per-hectare yields of any crop (~4–5 MT of oil per hectare versus 0.3–0.5 MT for soybean), has never scaled to anything approaching its potential. The National Mission on Edible Oils — Oil Palm (NMEO-OP), launched in 2021 with a target of expanding oil palm area to 10 lakh hectares by 2025–26, faces challenges of long gestation periods (oil palms take 5–7 years to reach commercial production), agro-climatic constraints, and the lack of a reliable cold-chain and processing infrastructure in potential cultivation areas like Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, and the Northeast.

Policy Levers and the Road Ahead

Reducing India’s edible oil import dependence requires action on three fronts simultaneously. On the supply side, India must invest far more aggressively in oilseed research, irrigation coverage for oilseed-growing regions, and procurement support for oilseed farmers. Raising soybean and mustard yields to even 60–70% of global best-practice levels would dramatically expand domestic production. Scaling up the NMEO-OP and solving its implementation bottlenecks is critical for long-term supply expansion.

On the demand side, India should encourage a gradual shift toward more diverse dietary fats, including domestic groundnut oil, sesame oil, and coconut oil, which are produced domestically in sufficient quantities for regional consumption. These oils have higher unit costs but also higher nutritional value and domestic supply resilience.

On the trade policy side, India should build a strategic edible oil reserve of at least 2–3 months of national consumption, financed through a modest cess on edible oil imports. Such a reserve would provide a meaningful buffer against the kind of supply shock that caused the 2021–22 price crisis, buying time for diplomatic and market interventions to take effect.

Conclusion

India’s edible oil import dependency is the product of decades of policy choices that prioritised cereal self-sufficiency over oilseed production — a choice that made sense in the food-insecure India of the 1960s and 1970s but has left the country structurally exposed to global commodity markets and geopolitical shocks in the twenty-first century. The 2021–22 oil price crisis was a warning. The window to act — through aggressive oilseed production enhancement, oil palm expansion, and strategic reserve creation — is open, but it will not remain so indefinitely. India’s kitchen economy, feeding 1.4 billion people three meals a day, deserves a more resilient foundation.
