Introduction: The Trillion-Dollar Trader with a Persistent Gap

India crossed the USD 1 trillion merchandise trade milestone in 2021–22, becoming only the eighth country in the world to achieve this threshold. Total merchandise exports reached approximately USD 422 billion in 2022–23, while imports crossed USD 714 billion — yielding a trade deficit of approximately USD 265 billion. To put this in perspective, India’s trade deficit alone is larger than the entire GDP of many middle-income countries. It represents a structural feature of the Indian economy that has persisted across decades, governments, and economic cycles — and understanding its anatomy is essential to formulating an effective trade and industrial policy for the next decade.

India’s trade trajectory over the past two decades has been one of dramatic expansion on both the import and export sides, but with imports consistently growing faster than exports. This asymmetry reflects a combination of structural factors: India’s energy import dependence, the concentration of manufactured exports in relatively low-value-added categories, the under-representation of India in global electronics and machinery value chains, and the peculiarities of India’s agricultural export policy, which alternates between liberalisation and intervention depending on domestic supply conditions.

This article examines India’s merchandise trade performance across its key dimensions — total flows, sectoral composition, the trade balance trajectory, and the specific cases of cereal exports, fertilizer imports, and petroleum trade — and draws out the policy implications for India’s ambition to become a leading trading nation.

The Macro Picture: A Trade Profile Shaped by Energy and Commodities

India’s import basket is dominated by a small number of energy and commodity categories. Petroleum crude and products consistently account for 25–30% of total import value. Precious metals and gems (primarily gold, imported for jewellery fabrication and investment) account for 8–10%. Electronic goods — smartphones, components, consumer durables — add another 10–12%. Fertilizers, edible oils, and machinery round out the top import categories.

The energy import bill is the single largest driver of India’s trade deficit and its most structurally stubborn component. India’s domestic crude oil production has been declining for years — the major oil fields at Bombay High are in natural decline — while consumption has been rising rapidly with motorisation and economic growth. At current trajectory, India’s crude oil import dependence (already at 85% of consumption) will continue to rise. Every USD 10 per barrel increase in the crude oil price adds approximately USD 14–15 billion to India’s annual import bill — a sensitivity that makes the trade balance highly exposed to geopolitical shocks in the Middle East and elsewhere.

India’s export basket has evolved significantly over the past two decades. Petroleum products — refined products exported from India’s world-class refining complex (particularly Reliance Industries’ Jamnagar refinery, the world’s largest) — have become the single largest export category, accounting for approximately 14–16% of total merchandise exports. This is a remarkable transformation: India has leveraged its refining capacity to become a net exporter of petroleum products even as it imports the crude oil feedstock. The “refinery margin” — the spread between crude input costs and refined product export realisations — is essentially a value-added manufacturing activity embedded in the petroleum trade.

Beyond petroleum products, India’s key export categories include engineering goods, chemicals, pharmaceuticals, textiles and apparel, and agricultural products (principally rice, seafood, and spices). The pharmaceutical sector has grown into a global force — India is the world’s largest generic medicines supplier — and represents genuine high-value, innovation-driven exports. But it remains a relatively small share of total trade volumes compared to the low-value-added categories.

Cereal Exports: India as the World’s Rice Granary

India’s cereal export performance over the past two decades has been remarkable in its scale and concentrated in its composition. India is the world’s single largest rice exporter, accounting for approximately 40% of global rice trade in recent years. In 2022–23, India exported approximately 22 million tonnes of rice — more than the next three exporters (Thailand, Vietnam, and Pakistan) combined. This export supremacy reflects India’s large production base, competitive farm-gate prices, and the diversity of rice varieties grown across the country (non-basmati white rice for mass markets, basmati rice for premium markets in the Middle East, Europe, and North America).

The data shows a clear evolution in the composition of cereal exports. Wheat exports, which were significant in 2021–22 when India had bumper stocks and global supply was disrupted by the Ukraine war, were subsequently banned as domestic prices rose — a pattern that India has repeated several times over the past decade. Maize exports have been modest but growing, driven by expanding feed and starch industry demand globally. The government’s 2023 initiative to promote millets internationally — India hosted the International Year of Millets 2023 — may generate new export opportunities in niche premium markets, but volumes remain small.

The recurrent tension in India’s cereal export policy is between two legitimate objectives: generating export earnings and farmer income through market access, and controlling domestic food prices by keeping cereals within the country. When international prices are high, the pressure to ban exports increases — but export bans impose losses on farmers and damage India’s reliability as a trading partner. The ideal policy response would be to maintain export freedom but use domestic stock releases from government warehouses to moderate domestic prices — a more surgical approach than blanket export bans.

The Fertilizer Import Dependence: A Structural Trade Liability

India’s fertilizer imports represent one of the clearest cases of structural trade dependence in the country’s import basket. The country imports approximately USD 10–15 billion worth of fertilizers annually — roughly 2–3% of total imports — with significant year-to-year variation depending on international prices.

The 2021–22 price spike illustrated the fiscal dimensions of fertilizer import dependence dramatically. With urea prices crossing USD 900/MT and DAP prices approaching USD 1,000/MT, India’s fertilizer import bill approximately tripled — from roughly USD 7 billion in 2019–20 to nearly USD 21 billion in 2021–22. Since the government absorbs the entire price difference between international prices and subsidised retail prices, this effectively transferred the trade shock directly to the fiscal deficit.

From a trade policy perspective, reducing fertilizer import dependence requires investment in domestic manufacturing capacity (particularly for urea, where India has made progress through plant revivals and new builds), raw material security for phosphates (through long-term contracts with Moroccan and Jordanian producers), and demand-side efficiency improvements (better soil testing, precision application, and the development of nano-urea and bio-fertilizers as partial substitutes).

Petroleum Trade: India’s Biggest Import and Its Largest Export

The peculiar structure of India’s petroleum trade — crude oil is its largest import; petroleum products are its largest export — gives India’s merchandise trade an unusual character. India imports approximately 200–220 million tonnes of crude oil annually and exports approximately 60–70 million tonnes of refined petroleum products. The net petroleum trade balance is negative (India is a substantial net buyer in value terms), but the export revenue from refined products represents a significant economic contribution.

The Russia–Ukraine war created an unusual opportunity for India. As Western buyers moved away from Russian crude, India significantly increased its purchases of discounted Russian Urals crude — at times paying USD 20–30 per barrel below Brent crude prices. Indian refiners processed this cheap feedstock and exported the refined products to European markets that had banned Russian petroleum products. This triangular trade was enormously profitable for Indian refiners in 2022–23 and contributed to a significant improvement in India’s merchandise trade balance relative to what would have been expected given global oil prices.

However, this window of opportunity is not structurally permanent. As Western sanctions regimes evolve and Russian crude finds alternative outlets (primarily China), the discount on Russian crude available to India has been narrowing. India’s refinery sector needs to continue upgrading its technology and expanding its capacity to process a diverse range of crude grades.

The Export Ambition: What Would It Take?

India has articulated an ambition to achieve USD 1 trillion in merchandise exports by 2030. Given that exports stood at approximately USD 420–450 billion in 2022–23, reaching USD 1 trillion in seven years would require a compound annual growth rate of approximately 12–13% — considerably faster than India’s recent export growth.

Achieving this ambition requires a fundamental upgrade in the composition, not just the volume, of India’s exports. The key challenge is India’s under-representation in manufactured goods exports. China’s exports are approximately five times India’s in value terms, driven overwhelmingly by electronics, machinery, and industrial goods — categories where India’s presence is minimal. India needs to attract investment in electronics manufacturing (the Production Linked Incentive scheme for mobile phones is a beginning but only a beginning), develop supply chains in capital goods and machinery, and improve the competitiveness of its textile and apparel sector against Bangladesh and Vietnam.

Agricultural exports — including processed foods, spices, marine products, and dairy — represent an area of genuine competitive advantage for India. The growth in agricultural export value from approximately USD 30 billion (2013–14) to over USD 50 billion (2022–23) is encouraging. But this growth needs to be accompanied by improvement in food safety standards, traceability systems, and quality certification — all areas where India has faced challenges with export bans from the EU and other markets.

Trade Deficit Management: The Currency Dimension

India’s persistent trade deficit has implications beyond the trade balance itself. A large deficit must be financed — by capital inflows (foreign direct investment, portfolio investment, remittances from the Indian diaspora, and external commercial borrowing). Remittances, which have reached USD 100–110 billion annually, are India’s single largest source of foreign exchange and provide a critical buffer against trade deficit financing pressure.

The rupee-dollar exchange rate is an important variable in trade dynamics. A weaker rupee makes Indian exports cheaper in dollar terms (benefiting exporters) but increases the domestic currency cost of imports (adding to the import bill and contributing to inflation). The RBI’s exchange rate management has historically sought to prevent excessive rupee depreciation while allowing gradual adjustment — a delicate balance in an environment of global capital flow volatility.

Conclusion

India’s trade story is one of genuine achievement — from a minor participant in global trade to the world’s 10th largest trading nation — but also of structural challenges that will determine whether India can fulfil its ambition to become a major manufacturing and export powerhouse. Reducing the structural trade deficit requires not just export promotion but a fundamental transformation of India’s industrial base, supply chain participation, and trade policy architecture. The data on cereal exports, fertilizer imports, and petroleum trade tells a nuanced story: India is competitive in some areas (rice exports, refined petroleum), vulnerable in others (fertilizers, electronics), and still finding its footing in the manufactured goods categories that will define 21st-century trade leadership.
