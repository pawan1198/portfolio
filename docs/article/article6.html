Introduction: Feeding the Fields That Feed the Nation

India’s agricultural miracle — the Green Revolution and its subsequent harvests — was built on three pillars: high-yielding variety seeds, irrigation, and chemical fertilizers. Of these three, fertilizers remain the most geopolitically exposed and fiscally costly. India is the world’s second-largest consumer of chemical fertilizers, using approximately 65–70 million nutrient tonnes (MT) of nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium annually to sustain the productivity of its 140 million hectares of cultivated land. Yet despite decades of investment in domestic manufacturing, India imports a significant share of its fertilizer requirement — and for certain nutrients, it is almost entirely dependent on foreign sources.

The consequences of this dependence became painfully visible in 2021–22. A perfect storm of post-pandemic demand recovery, Chinese export restrictions on urea and phosphate fertilizers, and the Russia–Ukraine war drove fertilizer prices to multi-decade highs. India’s fertilizer subsidy bill — which the government pays to protect farmers from world price fluctuations — crossed ₹2.5 lakh crore (approximately USD 30 billion) in a single year. This was not merely a budget line item; it was a fiscal emergency that forced a fundamental reassessment of India’s fertilizer policy architecture.

This article traces India’s fertilizer import trajectory, examines the geopolitical concentration risks inherent in its current supply chains, analyses the price shock mechanism and subsidy transmission, and identifies structural reforms that can reduce India’s vulnerability in the years ahead.

The Fertilizer Import Portfolio: NPK and Its Dependencies

Chemical fertilizers supply three primary macronutrients — nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), and potassium (K) — each of which India sources through a distinct combination of domestic production and imports.

Nitrogen (Urea): Large but Manageable Domestic Capacity

Urea is the most widely used nitrogenous fertilizer in India, accounting for approximately 50% of total fertilizer consumption by volume. India has a substantial domestic urea manufacturing base — approximately 28–30 MT annual capacity from 30+ plants. In recent years, the government has revived several idle gas-based plants (including at Gorakhpur, Ramagundam, and Sindri) under the New Investment Policy, adding several million tonnes of new capacity. As a result, India now produces approximately 25–27 MT of urea domestically and imports the remaining 6–9 MT.

The import sourcing pattern for urea has been geopolitically interesting. China was historically the dominant supplier, typically providing 30–50% of India’s urea imports. However, China imposed sharp export restrictions in late 2021 to protect domestic supplies, cutting off a major supply source at the worst possible moment — just as global prices were spiking. This forced India to rapidly diversify toward Oman (through OMIFCO, a joint venture in which Indian companies hold significant stakes), Iran, Egypt, and Russia.

This diversification, while forced, has arguably left India in a stronger structural position than before — more geographically balanced sourcing reduces single-country dependency risk. However, three of India’s four major urea suppliers (Iran, Russia, Egypt) face varying degrees of sanctions, political instability, or energy-cost volatility, meaning the diversification is less robust than it superficially appears.

Phosphorus (DAP, Rock Phosphate, Phosphoric Acid): The North African Dependency

Phosphorus-based fertilizers are India’s most concentrated import risk. India has almost no domestic phosphate rock reserves of economic significance — unlike nitrogen, which can be synthesised from atmospheric air using natural gas, or potassium, which at least has some domestic sources in Rajasthan (though of limited economic viability). India’s phosphate supply chain is therefore built entirely on imports.

The hierarchy runs as follows: India imports rock phosphate (primarily from Jordan and Morocco) as raw material for domestic phosphoric acid and single super phosphate (SSP) manufacturing; imports phosphoric acid (primarily from Morocco and Jordan) as an intermediate for DAP production in India; and imports finished DAP (primarily from Saudi Arabia, China, and Morocco) when domestic manufacturing cannot meet demand.

Morocco alone holds approximately 70–75% of the world’s known phosphate reserves. Jordan holds much of the rest. This geographic concentration of a strategically critical nutrient in two countries — neither of which is fully stable politically — represents a long-run vulnerability for Indian agriculture. India’s phosphate diplomacy with Morocco’s OCP Group (the world’s largest phosphate company) and Jordan’s JPMC is consequently of significant strategic importance, even if it rarely makes headlines.

Potassium (MOP): The Most Vulnerable Nutrient

Muriate of Potash (MOP) is India’s most exposed fertilizer import. India produces essentially zero potassium domestically. Every kilogramme of MOP applied to Indian fields — and India uses approximately 4–5 MT per year — must be imported. The sourcing geography has historically been dominated by Belarus (Belaruskali, one of the world’s largest potash producers), Canada (Nutrien, formerly Potash Corp), and Russia (Uralkali).

The 2022 geopolitical crisis turned this vulnerability into a crisis. Western sanctions on Belarus, imposed in response to the Lukashenko government’s political repression, cut off Belaruskali exports from the global financial system. Russia’s invasion of Ukraine triggered broader commodity disruption. The result was a near-tripling of MOP prices in 2021–22, from approximately USD 200/MT to over USD 600/MT. India’s government-to-government diplomacy — including a controversial direct deal with Belaruskali negotiated outside the formal sanctions framework — provided some relief, but the episode exposed the complete absence of strategic potash reserves or supply alternatives.

The Price Shock of 2021–22: A Fiscal Emergency

The fertilizer price crisis of 2021–22 was the most severe in India’s post-Independence agricultural history. The international price of urea, which had been USD 250–300/MT for much of 2019–20, crossed USD 900/MT at its peak in late 2021. DAP prices, which were USD 300–350/MT in 2020, reached USD 900–1,000/MT. MOP prices tripled from USD 200/MT to over USD 600/MT.

In normal years, India’s fertilizer subsidy bill runs to approximately ₹70,000–80,000 crore. In 2021–22, it crossed ₹2.5 lakh crore — more than three times the normal level — as the government absorbed the price differential between international prices and the controlled retail prices paid by farmers. This extraordinary expenditure crowded out other budgetary priorities and strained the fiscal deficit targets.

India’s fertilizer pricing architecture creates this fiscal risk structurally. Retail fertilizer prices — particularly for urea — are fixed by the government and have not been revised upward meaningfully in over a decade. The “Maximum Retail Price” of urea is approximately ₹5.36 per kg, while its economic cost (at 2021–22 international prices) was approximately ₹30–35 per kg. The entire gap is borne by the government through the fertilizer subsidy scheme. This system, while protecting farmers from price volatility in the short run, creates perverse incentives — excessive urea use (India over-applies urea relative to phosphorus and potassium, distorting the N:P:K ratio), soil degradation, and groundwater contamination — while also creating a massive, unhedged fiscal exposure to global commodity markets.

Structural Reform: What India Needs to Do

Addressing India’s fertilizer vulnerability requires action on multiple fronts.

Expanding domestic urea capacity is the first priority. The completed revival of the five sick urea plants has been helpful, but the pipeline needs to continue. New urea plants, particularly using coal-to-urea technology in coal-rich states like Jharkhand and Odisha, could reduce import dependence further — though this comes at the cost of higher carbon emissions and may conflict with India’s climate commitments.

Developing strategic fertilizer reserves is urgently needed. China maintains a 6–9 month fertilizer reserve; India holds barely a month’s worth of buffer stock. Creating a 3-month reserve of all three key fertilizers (urea, DAP, MOP) would provide a meaningful buffer against supply shocks and price spikes, reducing the pressure to accept unfavourable terms in crisis procurement.

Diversifying potash sourcing requires diplomatic investment. India needs to engage more deeply with Canadian producers, explore the viability of Jordanian potash, and develop relationships with emerging potash producers in Ethiopia and Congo. The government’s agricultural attaché offices at key embassies should be working proactively on this.

Reforming the urea subsidy architecture is the most politically difficult but most important structural change. Transitioning from a fixed-price subsidy to a direct benefit transfer (DBT) system — where farmers receive cash support and buy fertilizers at market prices — would eliminate the perverse incentives created by the current system, reduce over-application of urea, and decouple the government’s fiscal exposure from international price volatility. The Neem Coating of Urea initiative (introduced in 2015 to slow urea dissolution and reduce diversion) was a modest first step; the broader pricing reform remains unfinished business.

Investing in fertilizer-efficient crop varieties and soil health can reduce the absolute quantity of fertilizer needed. India’s fertilizer use efficiency — the kilograms of additional crop output per kilogram of nutrient applied — is well below global best practice. Better soil testing, precision application technologies, and the promotion of organic/bio-fertilizer supplementation can improve efficiency without reducing yields, gradually reducing the volume of imports required.

The Geopolitical Dimension: Fertilizers as a National Security Issue

The fertilizer crisis of 2021–22 should be viewed not merely as a commodity market event but as a national security stress test. Food production depends on fertilizers; if fertilizer supplies are cut off or priced out of reach for an extended period, Indian crop yields would fall, food prices would surge, and rural livelihoods would be devastated.

India’s geopolitical situation — with complex relationships with China (historically its largest urea supplier), Russia and Belarus (potash), and the Middle East/North Africa (phosphates and DAP) — means that fertilizer procurement is inescapably entangled with foreign policy. The government has shown pragmatism in this regard: India did not join the Western sanctions against Belarus or Russia and continued to negotiate fertilizer supplies through government-to-government channels. This realpolitik approach has helped manage the immediate supply crisis but does not address the structural dependency.

A deeper strategic investment in domestic production capacity, alternative supply relationships, and demand-side efficiency improvements is the only durable solution.

Conclusion

India’s fertilizer import dependency is a complex web of geological constraints (zero potash and limited phosphate reserves), policy distortions (urea subsidies that encourage over-use), and geopolitical exposures (concentration in a few supplier countries). The 2021–22 price crisis was the most expensive signal yet that this architecture is unsustainable. Reforming it requires political courage — particularly around urea pricing — as well as sustained investment in domestic manufacturing, strategic reserves, and soil health. The alternative — waiting for the next price shock to impose even greater fiscal and food security costs — is not an option that India’s agriculture, economy, or 700 million farming-dependent citizens can afford.
