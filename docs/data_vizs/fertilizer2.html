Overview

India is one of the world’s largest consumers and importers of chemical fertilizers. With its cultivated area of ~140 million hectares and a growing population, ensuring a steady supply of nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), and potassium (K) is a matter of national food security. This analysis covers urea, DAP (diammonium phosphate), MOP (muriate of potash), rock phosphate, and phosphoric acid imports from 2016 onward — examining volumes, source diversification, international price dynamics, and domestic production capacity.

TipKey Findings at a Glance
  • 🌾 Urea dominates nitrogen supply: India imports 6–9 million tonnes annually, with China historically the top supplier
  • 🌍 Morocco controls phosphate chain: Jordan, Morocco, and Saudi Arabia together supply most of India’s DAP and rock phosphate
  • 💊 MOP is the least diversified: Belarus, Canada, and Russia account for the vast majority of potash imports
  • 📈 Price shocks in 2021–22: Urea, DAP, and MOP prices each more than doubled during the Russia–Ukraine commodity crisis
  • 🏭 Domestic urea production is near capacity ceiling: India has expanded urea manufacturing, but imports remain essential
  • ⚖️ DAP production lags demand: India imports 3–4 MT of DAP annually despite having some domestic capacity

## ── Reusable interactive stacked bar ──────────────────────────────────────
fert_bar <- function(data, title, subtitle, palette_opt = "D") {
  data <- as.data.table(data)
  data[, tip := paste0("<b>", Country, "</b><br>",
                       "Year: ", Year, "<br>",
                       "Quantity: ", round(Qty, 2), " MMT<br>",
                       "Share: ", Percent, "%")]

  p <- ggplot(data, aes(x = Year, y = Qty, fill = Country,
                        tooltip = tip,
                        data_id = paste(Country, Year))) +
    geom_bar_interactive(stat = "identity", position = "stack",
                         width = 0.75, alpha = 0.9) +
    geom_text(
      aes(label = ifelse(Percent >= 8, paste0(Percent, "%"), "")),
      position = position_stack(vjust = 0.5),
      size = 3, color = "white", fontface = "bold"
    ) +
    scale_fill_viridis(discrete = TRUE, option = palette_opt,
                       begin = 0.1, end = 0.9) +
    scale_y_continuous(labels = comma_format()) +
    labs(title = title, subtitle = subtitle,
         y = "Quantity (Million Metric Tonnes)",
         x = "Year",
         caption = "Source: Ministry of Commerce, India | Analysis: Pawan") +
    my_theme

  girafe(ggobj = p, width_svg = 11, height_svg = 6, options = std_opts)
}

## Helper: filter to country-level, aggregate, group, and calculate shares
prep_fert <- function(dt, major_countries, country_renames = NULL,
                      year_cutoff = "2015") {
  ## Rename countries if needed
  if (!is.null(country_renames)) {
    for (i in seq_along(country_renames)) {
      dt[Country == names(country_renames)[i], Country := country_renames[i]]
    }
  }
  out <- dt[Country != "Total",
            .(Qty = sum(as.numeric(ImportMonth), na.rm = TRUE)),
            by = .(Country, Year)]
  ## Group minor countries
  out[!Country %in% major_countries, Country := "OTHER COUNTRY"]
  out <- out[, .(Qty = sum(Qty)), by = .(Country, Year)]
  out[, Qty := Qty / 100000]   ## tonnes → million metric tonnes
  out <- out[Year > year_cutoff]
  out[, Percent := round(Qty * 100 / sum(Qty)), by = Year]
  out
}

3 Urea Imports

Urea is the workhorse of India’s nitrogen fertilizer sector. China was historically the dominant supplier, but geopolitical shifts and India’s efforts to diversify have brought Oman, Iran, and Egypt into greater prominence.

Code

  subtitle = "Annual import volumes 2016–2024 (Million Metric Tonnes)",
  palette_opt = "D"

NoteUrea Supply Geography

China’s dominance as a urea supplier to India peaked around 2016–17. Since then, India has strategically diversified toward Oman (Oman India Fertiliser Company — OMIFCO, a joint venture), Iran, and Russia. This diversification provides some buffer against Chinese export restrictions, which Beijing has imposed intermittently.

4 DAP Imports

DAP (Di-Ammonium Phosphate) provides both nitrogen and phosphorus to crops. India’s domestic DAP production covers only ~40% of demand, necessitating large-scale imports.

7 MOP (Muriate of Potash) Imports

India imports nearly all its potash requirement. The 2022 Belarus sanctions and Russia–Ukraine war caused a severe MOP supply squeeze globally — India had to negotiate emergency supplies directly with Canada and Russia.

10 Key Takeaways

ImportantSummary of Findings

  1. India’s fertilizer import dependence is structural: Domestic production covers demand for urea, but phosphate and potash inputs are almost entirely imported.

  2. Supply concentration is a critical vulnerability: 2–3 countries control 80–90% of each fertilizer category’s supply to India. Export restrictions or geopolitical crises in any of these countries cause immediate supply and price shocks.

  3. The 2021–22 global price spike was the most severe fertilizer price event in decades, more than doubling DAP and MOP prices and pushing India’s subsidy bill to record levels.

  4. China’s urea export restrictions in 2021–22 forced India to diversify urea sourcing to Oman, Iran, Egypt, and Russia — a positive structural change, albeit forced.

  5. Potash is the most vulnerable input: India has zero domestic reserves of potassium and relies entirely on imports — primarily from Belarus, Canada, and Russia, all subject to geopolitical disruption risk.

  6. Domestic urea capacity has grown significantly since 2014 as several gas-based plants were revived/built, but the country still imports 6–9 MMT annually.

::: {.callout-note title="Urea Supply Geography"}
China's dominance as a urea supplier to India peaked around 2016–17. Since then, India has strategically diversified toward **Oman** (Oman India Fertiliser Company — OMIFCO, a joint venture), **Iran**, and **Russia**. This diversification provides some buffer against Chinese export restrictions, which Beijing has imposed intermittently.
:::

## Key Takeaways

::: {.callout-important title="Summary of Findings"}
1. **India's fertilizer import dependence is structural**: Domestic production covers demand for urea, but phosphate and potash inputs are almost entirely imported.

2. **Supply concentration is a critical vulnerability**: 2–3 countries control 80–90% of each fertilizer category's supply to India. Export restrictions or geopolitical crises in any of these countries cause immediate supply and price shocks.

3. **The 2021–22 global price spike** was the most severe fertilizer price event in decades, more than doubling DAP and MOP prices and pushing India's subsidy bill to record levels.

4. **China's urea export restrictions** in 2021–22 forced India to diversify urea sourcing to Oman, Iran, Egypt, and Russia — a positive structural change, albeit forced.

5. **Potash is the most vulnerable input**: India has zero domestic reserves of potassium and relies entirely on imports — primarily from Belarus, Canada, and Russia, all subject to geopolitical disruption risk.

6. **Domestic urea capacity has grown** significantly since 2014 as several gas-based plants were revived/built, but the country still imports 6–9 MMT annually.
:::
