Overview

India’s foodgrain supply chain is one of the largest and most complex public food economy systems in the world. It links 140 million farming households at one end to 800 million subsidy beneficiaries at the other — through a sequence of MSP procurement, FCI storage, buffer stock management, PDS distribution, and selective open market sales or exports. This analysis brings together three previously separate datasets — procurement records, FCI stock positions, and trade statistics — to trace the complete chain and identify where the system creates value and where it generates costs and inefficiencies.

TipKey Questions Addressed
  • How has procurement grown relative to buffer stock norms?
  • What is the relationship between stock build-up and export decisions?
  • How have India’s cereal export/import patterns tracked against domestic stock cycles?
  • Which crops show the tightest (and loosest) supply chain efficiency?

NoteReading the Integrated Table

The table traces the wheat supply chain end-to-end: procurement in the April–June rabi season builds up stocks visible in April-month stock positions, and excess stocks are released through exports (when permitted) or domestic open market sales (OMSS). Years of high wheat exports (e.g. 2021–22) correspond to years of surplus stocks built from strong prior procurement.

7 Key Takeaways

ImportantIntegrated Supply Chain Findings

  1. Procurement has grown faster than buffer norms: wheat procurement crossed 40 LMT and paddy exceeded 600 LMT in recent seasons — well above the buffer stock norms set by the government, creating storage pressure and fiscal carrying costs.

  2. The procurement-stocks-export chain is empirically visible: the scatter plot shows a strong positive relationship between annual paddy procurement and subsequent rice stock positions.

  3. India uses surplus stocks as an export safety valve: in years of strong wheat stocks (2021–22), India permitted large wheat exports; it reversed this with an export ban when domestic prices rose.

  4. Coarse grain procurement remains minimal despite rising domestic millet consumption and the government’s International Year of Millets 2023 push.

  5. The COVID-19 period demonstrated the system’s value: large pre-pandemic stocks enabled PMGKAY free grain distribution to 800 million people for over two years — a genuine food security achievement enabled by the buffer stock system.

## Key Takeaways

::: {.callout-important title="Integrated Supply Chain Findings"}
1. **Procurement has grown faster than buffer norms**: wheat procurement crossed 40 LMT and paddy exceeded 600 LMT in recent seasons — well above the buffer stock norms set by the government, creating storage pressure and fiscal carrying costs.

2. **The procurement-stocks-export chain is empirically visible**: the scatter plot shows a strong positive relationship between annual paddy procurement and subsequent rice stock positions.

3. **India uses surplus stocks as an export safety valve**: in years of strong wheat stocks (2021–22), India permitted large wheat exports; it reversed this with an export ban when domestic prices rose.

4. **Coarse grain procurement remains minimal** despite rising domestic millet consumption and the government's International Year of Millets 2023 push.

5. **The COVID-19 period demonstrated the system's value**: large pre-pandemic stocks enabled PMGKAY free grain distribution to 800 million people for over two years — a genuine food security achievement enabled by the buffer stock system.
:::
