Overview

Food prices affect the welfare of every Indian household. This analysis examines retail and wholesale price trajectories for four commodity groups — cereals, pulses, edible oils, and vegetables — drawing on government price monitoring data. By comparing retail with wholesale prices and tracking commodity-level trends, we can identify where prices are diverging, where the supply chain spreads are widest, and which commodities have seen the sharpest inflationary pressure.

TipKey Findings at a Glance
  • 🌾 Cereal prices are the most stable: supported by MSP procurement, PDS distribution, and strategic stock releases
  • 🫘 Pulses show the highest price volatility: arhar (tur) dal has repeatedly hit ₹150–200/kg in recent years
  • 🫙 Edible oil prices are import-driven: surged 80–100% in 2021–22 with global commodity price spikes
  • 🍅 Vegetable prices are the most erratic: tomatoes can rise 5–10× within a single season before crashing
  • 📊 Retail-wholesale spreads are widest for pulses and vegetables: indicating supply chain inefficiencies
  • 🔄 Price transmission is asymmetric: wholesale increases pass through rapidly; decreases are slow and incomplete

2 Data Loading and Cleaning

Code 
## Load parquet datasets
retail    <- open_dataset("~/foodprices/retailprices/parquetfilesnew/")
wholesale <- open_dataset("~/foodprices/wholesaleprices/parquetfilesnew/")

retail    <- as.data.table(select(retail,    c(states, date, commodity, price, commodity_group)))
wholesale <- as.data.table(select(wholesale, c(states, date, commodity, price, commodity_group)))

retail$Type    <- "Retail"
wholesale$Type <- "Wholesale"
wholesale$price <- wholesale$price / 100   ## Convert paise to rupees if needed

combined <- rbind(retail, wholesale)

## Clean price
combined[, price := ifelse(is.na(price), 0, price)]

## Clean commodity names
combined[, commodity := gsub(" @|\\(\\)|\\*|Packed", "", commodity)]
combined[, commodity := trimws(gsub("[-/]+", "-", commodity))]

## Remove aggregate rows
combined <- combined[!states %in% c("Maximum Price","Minimum Price",
                                    "Modal Price","Average Price")]
combined <- combined[price > 0]

## ── Helper: national average price trend ─────────────────────────────────
nat_avg <- function(dt, grp, type_filter) {
  dt[commodity_group == grp & Type == type_filter,
     .(avg_price = mean(price, na.rm = TRUE),
       med_price = median(price, na.rm = TRUE)),
     by = .(date, commodity)]
}

3 Cereals

NoteWhy Cereal Prices Are Stable

The government’s dual mandate of protecting farmer income (through MSP) and consumer welfare (through PDS at subsidised prices) creates a price floor and a price ceiling around cereals. This is the most heavily managed segment of India’s food economy.

3.1 Retail Cereal Prices

Code 
r1 <- combined[commodity_group == "Cereals" & Type == "Retail"]
r1_avg <- r1[, .(avg = mean(price, na.rm = TRUE)), by = .(date, commodity)]
r1_avg[, tip := paste0("<b>", commodity, "</b><br>",
                       "Date: ", format(date, "%b %Y"), "<br>",
                       "Avg retail price: ₹<b>", round(avg, 2), "</b>/kg")]

p_cr <- ggplot(r1_avg, aes(x = date, y = avg, color = commodity,
                            tooltip = tip, data_id = paste(commodity, date),
                            group = commodity)) +
  geom_smooth_interactive(method = "gam", se = FALSE, linewidth = 1.2) +
  facet_wrap(~ commodity, scales = "free_y") +
  scale_color_viridis(discrete = TRUE, option = "D") +
  scale_x_date(date_breaks = "1 year", date_labels = "%Y") +
  scale_y_continuous(labels = label_number(prefix = "₹")) +
  labs(title   = "Retail Cereal Prices (National Average)",
       subtitle = "GAM-smoothed trend — hover to see exact date and price",
       x = "Date", y = "Price (₹/kg)",
       caption = "Source: Price Monitoring Division, Dept. of Consumer Affairs | Analysis: Pawan") +
  my_theme + theme(legend.position = "none")

girafe(ggobj = p_cr, width_svg = 12, height_svg = 7, options = std_opts)

grp_avg[, tip := paste0("<b>", Type, " — ", commodity_group, "</b><br>",

       x = "Date", y = "Average Price (₹/unit)",
       caption = "Source: Price Monitoring Division | Analysis: Pawan") +
  my_theme

girafe(ggobj = p_comp, width_svg = 12, height_svg = 8, options = std_opts)
NoteInterpreting the Retail-Wholesale Spread

A wide and widening gap between retail and wholesale prices signals supply chain inefficiencies — excess intermediaries, poor market connectivity, or rent-seeking at some point in the distribution chain. A narrowing gap typically indicates improved market integration through better roads, digital price discovery, or direct retail procurement from farmers. Pulses and vegetables consistently show the largest spreads.

8 Key Takeaways

ImportantSummary of Findings

  1. Cereals are the most price-stable food group, reflecting the government’s comprehensive procurement, buffer stock, and subsidised distribution architecture under the NFSA.

  2. Pulses are the most volatile major food group: their volatility stems from weather-sensitive rainfed cultivation, limited buffer stock management historically, and episodic import duty adjustments.

  3. Edible oil prices in India are essentially determined by global markets: the 2021–22 commodity price spike is unmistakably visible in the retail price series, with prices roughly doubling before retreating.

  4. Vegetable price volatility is a chronic structural problem: tomato, onion, and potato (“TOP” commodities) collectively drive a disproportionate share of food CPI volatility, yet the policy response remains largely reactive.

  5. Retail-wholesale spreads are widest for pulses and vegetables, suggesting these are the segments where investment in supply chain infrastructure, cold storage, and market linkages would yield the highest consumer welfare benefits.

  6. Price transmission is consistently asymmetric: increases in wholesale prices pass through to retail prices quickly; decreases are absorbed slowly, systematically benefiting intermediaries at consumers’ expense.

## Retail vs. Wholesale Comparison

The following plots compare average retail and wholesale prices within each commodity group, revealing the **supply chain spread** — a proxy for distribution, storage, and intermediary costs.

```{r rw-comparison}
## Build retail-wholesale comparison for each group
grp_avg <- combined[
  commodity_group %in% c("Cereals", "Pulses", "Edible oils", "Vegetables"),
  .(avg_price = mean(price, na.rm = TRUE)),
  by = .(date, Type, commodity_group)
]
grp_avg[, tip := paste0("<b>", Type, " — ", commodity_group, "</b><br>",
                        "Date: ", format(date, "%b %Y"), "<br>",
                        "Avg price: ₹<b>", round(avg_price, 2), "</b>")]

p_comp <- ggplot(grp_avg, aes(x = date, y = avg_price, color = Type,
                               tooltip = tip,
                               data_id = paste(Type, commodity_group, date),
                               group = Type)) +
  geom_line_interactive(linewidth = 1.2, alpha = 0.85) +
  facet_wrap(~ commodity_group, scales = "free_y", ncol = 2) +
  scale_color_manual(values = c("Retail" = "#e74c3c", "Wholesale" = "#2980b9"),
                     labels = c("Retail" = "Retail Price", "Wholesale" = "Wholesale Price")) +
  scale_x_date(date_breaks = "1 year", date_labels = "%Y") +
  scale_y_continuous(labels = label_number(prefix = "₹")) +
  labs(title   = "Retail vs. Wholesale Prices by Commodity Group",
       subtitle = "The gap between lines represents the distribution/intermediary margin",
       x = "Date", y = "Average Price (₹/unit)",
       caption = "Source: Price Monitoring Division | Analysis: Pawan") +
  my_theme

girafe(ggobj = p_comp, width_svg = 12, height_svg = 8, options = std_opts)
```

::: {.callout-note title="Interpreting the Retail-Wholesale Spread"}
A **wide and widening gap** between retail and wholesale prices signals supply chain inefficiencies — excess intermediaries, poor market connectivity, or rent-seeking at some point in the distribution chain. A **narrowing gap** typically indicates improved market integration through better roads, digital price discovery, or direct retail procurement from farmers. Pulses and vegetables consistently show the largest spreads.
:::

## Key Takeaways

::: {.callout-important title="Summary of Findings"}
1. **Cereals are the most price-stable food group**, reflecting the government's comprehensive procurement, buffer stock, and subsidised distribution architecture under the NFSA.

2. **Pulses are the most volatile major food group**: their volatility stems from weather-sensitive rainfed cultivation, limited buffer stock management historically, and episodic import duty adjustments.

3. **Edible oil prices in India are essentially determined by global markets**: the 2021–22 commodity price spike is unmistakably visible in the retail price series, with prices roughly doubling before retreating.

4. **Vegetable price volatility is a chronic structural problem**: tomato, onion, and potato ("TOP" commodities) collectively drive a disproportionate share of food CPI volatility, yet the policy response remains largely reactive.

5. **Retail-wholesale spreads are widest for pulses and vegetables**, suggesting these are the segments where investment in supply chain infrastructure, cold storage, and market linkages would yield the highest consumer welfare benefits.

6. **Price transmission is consistently asymmetric**: increases in wholesale prices pass through to retail prices quickly; decreases are absorbed slowly, systematically benefiting intermediaries at consumers' expense.
:::
