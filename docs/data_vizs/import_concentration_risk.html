Overview

India’s import dependence is well-documented, but the geopolitical concentration of that dependence — how many countries supply each critical import — is less frequently quantified. A high concentration means that a single country’s export restriction, natural disaster, or political crisis can immediately disrupt India’s supply. This analysis uses the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) — a standard measure of market concentration — to rank India’s import categories by geopolitical risk, and tracks how concentration has evolved over time.

HHI is calculated as the sum of squared shares: HHI = Σ(sᵢ²) where sᵢ is each supplier’s percentage share. Values range from 0 (perfectly diversified) to 10,000 (single supplier). By convention: <1,500 = low concentration; 1,500–2,500 = moderate; >2,500 = high concentration.

calc_hhi_fert <- function(dt, label) {
  out <- dt[Country!="Total",.(Qty=sum(as.numeric(ImportMonth),na.rm=TRUE)),by=.(Country,Year)]
  out <- out[Year>"2015"]
  out[,HHI:=hhi(Qty),by=Year]
  out[,.(HHI=first(HHI)),by=Year][,Category:=label] }

hhi_urea  <- calc_hhi_fert(t1,"Urea")
hhi_dap   <- calc_hhi_fert(t2,"DAP")
hhi_rock  <- calc_hhi_fert(t3,"Rock Phosphate")
hhi_phos  <- calc_hhi_fert(t4,"Phosphoric Acid")
hhi_mop   <- calc_hhi_fert(t5,"MOP (Potash)")
hhi_fert  <- rbindlist(list(hhi_urea,hhi_dap,hhi_rock,hhi_phos,hhi_mop))

oil_year_fn <- function(dt) {
  dt[,Month_num:=match(Month,month.abb)]
  dt[,Fin_Year:=ifelse(Month_num>10,paste0(Year,"-",substr(as.numeric(Year)+1,3,4)),
                       paste0(as.numeric(Year)-1,"-",substr(Year,3,4)))]
  dt }

palmoil_qty   <- oil_year_fn(readRDS("/home/pawan/edibleoils/palmoil_importqty.rds"))
soybeanoil_qty <- oil_year_fn(readRDS("/home/pawan/edibleoils/soybeanoil_importqty.rds"))

for(d in list(palmoil_qty,soybeanoil_qty,sunfloweroil_qty)) d[,ImportMonth:=gsub(",","",ImportMonth)]

calc_hhi_oil <- function(dt, label) {
  out <- dt[Country!="Total",.(Qty=sum(as.numeric(ImportMonth),na.rm=TRUE)),by=.(Country,Fin_Year)]
  out <- out[Fin_Year>="2010-11"&Fin_Year!="2024-25"]
  out[,HHI:=hhi(Qty),by=Fin_Year]
  out[,.(HHI=first(HHI)),by=Fin_Year][,Category:=label][,Year:=substr(Fin_Year,1,4)] }

hhi_palm <- calc_hhi_oil(palmoil_qty,"Palm Oil")
hhi_soy  <- calc_hhi_oil(soybeanoil_qty,"Soybean Oil")

hhi_oils <- rbindlist(list(hhi_palm[,.(Year,HHI,Category)],
                            hhi_soy[,.(Year,HHI,Category)],
                            hhi_sf[,.(Year,HHI,Category)]))

7 Policy Implications

ImportantConcentration Risk: Priority Actions by Category

Immediate priorities (HHI >3,000):

  • Phosphoric Acid: Diversify to Tunisia, Senegal; invest in long-term OCP/Morocco supply agreement with price stabilisation clauses.
  • MOP: Deepen Canada engagement; explore Laos and Turkmenistan as emerging potash sources; build 3-month strategic reserve.
  • Sunflower Oil: Develop Turkey, Argentina as alternative suppliers; consider establishing Black Sea alternative routing through Romania/Bulgaria.

Medium-term priorities (HHI 1,500–3,000):

  • Urea: Maintain post-2021 diversification; preserve OMIFCO relationship; develop Egypt and Iran supply channels contingent on sanctions landscape.
  • DAP: Expand Morocco OCP relationship; consider joint venture in Moroccan DAP manufacturing.

Structural actions:

  • Mandate minimum 6-month strategic reserves for all Category A critical imports (fertilizers, edible oils).
  • Develop a geopolitical supply risk index that is updated quarterly and formally incorporated into import policy decisions.

8 Key Takeaways

ImportantSummary of Concentration Analysis

  1. Phosphoric acid and MOP (potash) are India’s highest-risk import categories by HHI measure — both have effective single/duopoly suppliers and both faced severe disruptions in 2021–22.

  2. Sunflower oil carries the fastest-rising concentration risk: its share in the edible oil basket tripled over 14 years, and its supply is now over 80% dependent on the Ukraine-Russia corridor.

  3. Urea concentration has actually improved since 2021 — a case where geopolitical shock (China’s export restriction) forced beneficial diversification.

  4. Palm oil presents a stable duopoly — high concentration (Indonesia+Malaysia ~90%) but from two broadly stable partners with deep commercial relationships with India; the 2022 Indonesia export ban was short-lived.

  5. The 2021–22 commodity crisis was a wake-up call: multiple categories simultaneously experienced supply stress, revealing how India’s concentrated import structure translates into correlated fiscal and food security risks.

  6. A formal strategic reserves and supply diversification policy — modelled on practices in Japan, China, and the EU — is urgently needed for Category A critical imports.

## Policy Implications

::: {.callout-important title="Concentration Risk: Priority Actions by Category"}
**Immediate priorities (HHI >3,000)**:

- **Phosphoric Acid**: Diversify to Tunisia, Senegal; invest in long-term OCP/Morocco supply agreement with price stabilisation clauses.
- **MOP**: Deepen Canada engagement; explore Laos and Turkmenistan as emerging potash sources; build 3-month strategic reserve.
- **Sunflower Oil**: Develop Turkey, Argentina as alternative suppliers; consider establishing Black Sea alternative routing through Romania/Bulgaria.

**Medium-term priorities (HHI 1,500–3,000)**:

- **Urea**: Maintain post-2021 diversification; preserve OMIFCO relationship; develop Egypt and Iran supply channels contingent on sanctions landscape.
- **DAP**: Expand Morocco OCP relationship; consider joint venture in Moroccan DAP manufacturing.

**Structural actions**:

- Mandate minimum **6-month strategic reserves** for all Category A critical imports (fertilizers, edible oils).
- Develop **a geopolitical supply risk index** that is updated quarterly and formally incorporated into import policy decisions.
:::

## Key Takeaways

::: {.callout-important title="Summary of Concentration Analysis"}
1. **Phosphoric acid and MOP (potash) are India's highest-risk import categories** by HHI measure — both have effective single/duopoly suppliers and both faced severe disruptions in 2021–22.

2. **Sunflower oil carries the fastest-rising concentration risk**: its share in the edible oil basket tripled over 14 years, and its supply is now over 80% dependent on the Ukraine-Russia corridor.

3. **Urea concentration has actually improved** since 2021 — a case where geopolitical shock (China's export restriction) forced beneficial diversification.

4. **Palm oil presents a stable duopoly** — high concentration (Indonesia+Malaysia ~90%) but from two broadly stable partners with deep commercial relationships with India; the 2022 Indonesia export ban was short-lived.

5. **The 2021–22 commodity crisis was a wake-up call**: multiple categories simultaneously experienced supply stress, revealing how India's concentrated import structure translates into correlated fiscal and food security risks.

6. **A formal strategic reserves and supply diversification policy** — modelled on practices in Japan, China, and the EU — is urgently needed for Category A critical imports.
:::
